## Bottom Line

There is an immediate revenue opportunity worth an estimated +2-3% in at least ten markets by raising conventional avocado prices 5%, because demand in those cities barely drops when prices rise. Separately, a handful of mid-tier cities (Buffalo-Rochester, Pittsburgh, Indianapolis) are growing avocado sales 25-30% year-over-year and should receive more supply before competitors fill the gap.

## Key Findings

1. **Organic buyers are far more price-sensitive than conventional buyers.** A 10% price increase on conventional avocados costs roughly 8% in volume; the same increase on organic costs 11%. This means pricing strategies must differ by product type. *(Diagnostic Report, Section 1)*

2. **Ten inelastic markets can absorb a 5% price increase with net revenue gains.** Baltimore-Washington, Boston, Charlotte, Dallas-Fort Worth, and six others would see +1.5% to +3.6% more revenue per market with minimal volume loss. Even under pessimistic assumptions, all remain revenue-positive. *(Prescriptive Report, Section 1)*

3. **Mid-tier cities are the growth engine.** Buffalo-Rochester, Pittsburgh, and Grand Rapids are growing sales 25-30% annually, far outpacing the national average. Meanwhile, Portland, Dallas-Fort Worth, and Denver are declining. *(Predictive Report, Section 3)*

4. **Prices follow a reliable seasonal cycle.** Prices peak in September and trough in January-February. This pattern repeats consistently and can be exploited: raise prices during July-November when supply is tight, and run promotions in January-February and around Cinco de Mayo when volume naturally surges. *(Descriptive Report, Section 3; Prescriptive Report, Section 4)*

5. **San Francisco is the top opportunity for shifting shelf space toward organic.** The organic premium there is $0.81 per unit, and organic shoppers in that market are relatively insensitive to price. Los Angeles, San Diego, and Spokane also qualify. *(Prescriptive Report, Section 3)*

## Risks and Caveats

- **Price sensitivity estimates are correlational, not causal.** The data shows what happened historically, not what will happen if prices are deliberately changed. Small, controlled tests are recommended before broad rollouts. *(Diagnostic Report, Section 1; Prescriptive Report, Section 5)*
- **Volume forecasts are unreliable for half the markets.** Price forecasts are solid (accurate within 15-18% for most cities), but volume projections miss by 20-80% in many regions, especially for organic. Volume-dependent plans should be treated as directional. *(Predictive Report, Section 4)*
- **Data ends March 2018.** All forecasts project forward from that point. Structural market shifts since then are not captured.
- **No cost data available.** Revenue-maximizing prices are not necessarily profit-maximizing. Margin analysis requires cost-per-unit data not in this dataset.

## Next Steps

- Run controlled price tests in Baltimore-Washington and Boston (the two highest-confidence markets) to validate the revenue uplift estimates before broader rollout.
- Obtain cost-per-unit data to convert revenue recommendations into profit recommendations.
- Upgrade forecasting models to include seasonal components and external variables (weather, supply data), which would improve the unreliable volume forecasts.
- Refresh the analysis with current data to confirm that growth and decline trends identified here still hold.
